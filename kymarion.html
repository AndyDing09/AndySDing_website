Project Kymarion

An ISEF autonomous surface vehicle designed to map nearshore microplastic accumulation at current-convergence zones along the Massachusetts coast — using low-cost GPS-tagged sensors and hydrodynamic modeling to find where plastic piles up.

Competition ISEF
Category Environmental Science & Earth/Ocean Sciences
Role Co-Investigator
Timeline Summer 2026 – Summer 2027
Partner Ethan Zhang ↗
Status: Build and bench phase. The vehicle has moved to an on-boat pump-and-filter architecture (design V2.1), and the near-term goal is a calm-water mechanism shakedown on a local pond — navigate to waypoints, pump a measured volume, filter, and recover a lab-countable sample with flow, current, and GPS all logging together. The convergence-zone coastal study follows once the mechanism is proven, targeting the 2026–27 ISEF cycle.

The Problem

Microplastics are distributed unevenly across coastal waters, but predicting where they concentrate is hard. We hypothesize that sites where opposing nearshore currents converge act as natural traps — accumulating floating plastic debris the same way they accumulate biological matter. If the pattern is real and predictable, it opens the door to targeted capture station deployment rather than expensive, spray-and-pray ocean cleanup.

Existing research on coastal microplastic distribution relies on manual trawl and grab sampling, which is too sparse to characterize convergence-zone dynamics. We needed a cheap, repeatable, GPS-accurate survey platform that pumps a measured volume of water and filters it at each stop — so concentration comes out as particles per litre, not a jar you hope is representative. The study design pairs sites: 3 convergence zones against 3 distance-matched controls, compared with a matched-pair test.

The Vehicle

Kymarion is a low-cost trimaran ASV: two sealed HDPE pontoons on a 20 mm aluminum-extrusion frame, with a central, faired intake element that feeds the sampler. An ESP32 runs the whole boat — reading the sensors, driving the pump, and logging every reading to an SD card with a GPS timestamp. It's RC-driven for the first pond runs (isolating the pump-and-filter loop from autonomy bugs), with GPS + IMU waypoint navigation to follow.

Sampling & Filtration

Rather than dosing the water with dyes in the field — which is both fragile and illegal to discharge — Kymarion is a mechanical harvester. At each waypoint it pumps a measured volume of surface water through a staged mesh cascade, then the analytical mesh is pulled and counted in a lab. The staged cascade keeps the fine mesh from blinding on algae and organics:

  • Stage 1 — intake screen (~2 mm, 316 stainless): sheds leaves, twigs, and wildlife before water enters the pump.
  • Stage 2 — pre-filter (~800 µm–1 mm): catches coarse algae, duckweed, and sediment.
  • Stage 3 — analytical mesh (300 µm, 316 SS / PET): the count mesh, swapped one cartridge per site and sealed in a non-plastic container. Never nylon — nylon sheds its own fibers into a fiber-dominated count.

An INA219 watches the pump's current draw; when a clogging mesh makes the pump work harder, the ESP32 stops the pump and thrusters, lets the debris settle off the mesh, relocates a few metres, and resumes — without ever reversing flow across the captured sample. Concentration is normalized against the pumped volume: 

Concentration (particles/L) = Particle Count ÷ (Pumped Volume × Recovery Efficiency)

Back in the lab, organics are digested (KOH / wet-peroxide) and particles are counted under a microscope, with a hot-needle check and FTIR / Raman spectroscopy to confirm which particles are actually plastic. Field and procedural blanks control for airborne fiber contamination.

Study Design

  • Identify candidate convergence zones along the study coastline from public current, tidal, and bathymetric data plus direct observation of drift and debris accumulation.
  • Prove the pump-and-filter mechanism first on a calm local pond, then take the same mechanism to the coast.
  • Sample 6 waypoints per deployment as 3 matched pairs — each convergence site against a distance-matched control — across multiple tidal states.
  • Quantify microplastic concentration per site by lab microscopy, confirmed with FTIR / Raman.
  • Compute a convergence-strength metric for each site from local current-field data.
  • Test the hypothesis with a matched-pair comparison and regression of concentration against convergence strength, and map any confirmed accumulation hotspots.
